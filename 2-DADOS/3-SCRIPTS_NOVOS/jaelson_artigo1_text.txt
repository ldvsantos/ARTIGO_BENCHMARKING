SOILTILLAGEANDPREVIOUSCROPSONSOILATTRIBUTES INALONG-TERMPLOTIN NORTHEASTERN BRAZIL


ABSTRACT - Long-term studies on conventional and conservationist soil tillage systemsassociated with cover crops contribute significantly to understanding the behavior of soilphysical and chemical attributes in various environmental conditions. This study aimed toanalyze the effects of conventional tillage, minimum tillage, and no-tillage systems, combinedwithdifferentcovercrops,onsoilbehaviorinlong-termplots.ConductedattheRuralCampus Experimental Station of the Universidade Federal de Sergipe, the experiment hasbeen ongoing since 2001 in dystrophic Red-YellowArgissol. The area was cultivatedwithcornfordualpurposes(cobsaleandforageproduction)underConventionalTillage(CT), Minimum Tillage (MT), and No-Tillage (NT) systems, each combined with differentcover crops, including sunn hemp, millet, pigeon pea, and cowpea. Soil samples were collectedfrom the 0.0–0.10 m and 0.10–0.20 m layers. NT demonstrated a statistically higher soildensity in the 0.0–0.10 m layer compared to other systems. Conservationist systems hadhigher levels of Total Organic Carbon (TOC) at the soil surface. NT also showed significantlyhigher Soil Organic Matter (SOM) in both layers compared to CT and differed from othersystems in carbon stock levels. Among the cover crops, Sunn hemp yielded the best outcomesfor SOM, TOC, and carbon stock. Conservationist tillage systems, particularly those pairedwithlegumes ascover crops,can enhancecarbon stockand improvesoil quality.

INTRODUCTION


Soil has the capacity to promote the development of plant species, and consequently thestorage and recycling of nutrients (ATHAR E KANWAL, 2022). However, soil is a resourcewith a potentially fast degradation rate, but with a very slow process of regeneration andformation.TheenvironmentsoftheNortheasternCoastalTablelandspredominantlyoriginated from sedimentary rocks, present soil types which behave different in comparison toother tropical regions subjected to degradation by erosion processesandnutritionaldepletion,duetounsustainablesoilmanagement,leadingtoloworganicmatter content (MORAES et al., 2020).
Furthermore, high soil density values combined with soil resistance to penetration resultin reduced soil porosity, leading compacted soil layers limiting plant development, especiallythe root system (KAHLON; SINGH; DHINGRA, 2020), resulting in low crop production andlowwaterretentioncapacity. In addition, the type of soil plays an important role indensitybasedonitsmineralogicalnature,withagreaterorlesserfractionofclay(MOSSADEGHIBJÖRKLUND et al., 2019). Clayey soils tend to be more susceptible tocompaction due to their structure and lower porosity, which can result in greater soil density,increasing resistance to penetration and water infiltration (ZHAO et al., 2017).
Therefore, it is necessary to use soil and water conservation techniques, favoring theincrease of soil biomass and the amount of organic matter, promoting the improvement of thesoil'sphysicalpropertiesanditscropyieldcapacity (WANG etal.,2020).
Conservationist tillage systems such as Minimum tillage (MT) and No tillage (NT) tostructurally improve the soil, improving biological activity and increasing the content oforganicmatter.MinimumTillageincreasescarbonretentioninthesoil,reduceswaterevaporation, improves infiltration and promotes the gradual release of nutrients, and alsocontributes to increase the stock of organic carbon in the soil, consequently improving soilhealth and mitigating climate change.
No-Tillage requires the adoption of crop rotation in the area, in which the plants providethe soil with greater nutrient cycling and favoring the crops used in succession (SILVA et al.,2020). Leguminous species can contribute to a greater increase in soil organic matter, inaddition contributing to increased productivity (MUHAMMAD et al., 2019). The use ofsunn hemp (Sunn hemp juncea), pigeon pea (Cajanus cajan) and cowpea (Vigna unguiculata(L.)) demonstrates a high capacity to promote nutrient accumulation and greater biomassproduction.
The objective of this study was to evaluate the effects of conventional tillage, minimumtillage,and no-tillage systemswith different covercrops, on soilbehavior in long-termplots.


MATERIALSANDMETHODS


Descriptionofthestudyarea


The study was carried out at the Campus Rural Research Station, of the UniversidadeFederal de Sergipe (UFS) (coordinates are 10° 55' 24.390" S 37° 11' 57.920" W), located inmunicipality of São Cristóvão, Sergipe state, northeastern Brazil. Experiments with differentsoiltillagesystemsandpreviouscropshavebeenconductedsincetheyearof2001in soil classified as a dystrophic Red-Yellow Argissol(SANTOS, et al., 2018), in an area with an average altitude of 22 m above sea level, with aclimate type AS' - tropical rainy with dry summer following the KOPPEN classification andaverage rainfall of approximately 1200 mm per year, with the rainy season between April andSeptember.


Characterizationofthe experimentalarea anddatacollection

The experimental area has been managed since 2001, in long-term plots with differentsoil tillage systems (Conventional Tillage-CT, Minimum Tillage-MT, and No-Tillage-NT)using the succession of green corn (Zea mays L.) followed by cover crops such as sunn hemp(Sunn hempjunceaL.),pigeonpea(CajanuscajanL.(MILLSP)),millet(PennisetumglaucumL.) and cowpea(Vigna unguiculata). (Figure 1).



Figure 1. Sketch of the long-term plots at the Campus Rural Research Station.



For conventional tillage system (CT), a disc plow was used as a primary step and aleveling harrow as secondary step, with a turning of 30 and 10 cm respectively. In minimumtillage(MT),onlythelevelingharrowwasusedasprimaryandsecondarysteps,withacutting depth around 10 cm; and for No-tillage (NT), no plowing was used from the secondyear of cropping.
The experiment was organized in 12 plots, each experimental unit 60 m² (6m x 10m)size with a total of 36 subplots. Fertilization is carried out through the application of NPK intheformoftriplesuperphosphate,ureaandpotassiumchlorideindosesofN-120,P2O5–90and K 2 O - 110 kg. ha -1 and soil correction with liming, as necessary, based on the results ofsoil chemical analyses.
Soilsampleswerecollectedbeforecorn(ZeamaysL.)plantingandafterthedecomposition period of the previous crops, between March and April 2022. Small trencheswere opened within each plot, from where two deformed samples and one undisturbed samplewere collected at a depth of 0.0–0.10 m and 0.10–0.20 m, in a total of 24 samples per tillagesystem and 72 samples in the experimental area.
The samples were taken to the Erosion and Sedimentation Laboratory (LABES) of UFS,dried in ovens, de-clumped, sieved with a 2.0 mm mesh to obtain air-dry fine soil andanalyzed according to the methodology described in the Soil Analysis Manual and then sentfor analysis to the Soil Remediation Laboratory (LRS) of UFS to determine total organiccarbon via wet oxidation using the method Yeomans e Bremner, (1988). The organic matterstock was calculated using the soil density and the thickness of each evaluated layer and theBremner factor 1.724.


Statistical analysis


Analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to examine differences of Total OrganicCarbon (TOC) and Soil Organic Matter (SOM), carbon stock and soil bulk density accordingto different soil tillage system and previous crops. The Kolmogorov-Smirnov and Shapiro-Wilktestswereusedtoassessdatanormality(HANUSZ;TARASINSKA;ZIELINSKI,2016),whiletheassumptionofhomogeneityofvariancewasverifiedbyLevene'stest(SCHULTZ,1985).
Given the lack of homogeneity and normality in the data, we opted to use bootstrappingprocedures (1000 resamplings; 95% CI BCa), in order to reach greater reliability of the resultsand correction of deviations from the normality of the sample distribution, in addition toproviding a 95% confidence interval for the differences between the means. Furthermore, thebootstrap method does not require as many assumptions to estimate the parameters of thedistributions of interest; it generally provides more accurate answers; and does not depend onthe original distribution of the data.
The bootstrap confidence interval was estimated by fitting each of the 1000 data setsresampledfromtheoriginaldatasettothemodel.Whenthereisnoconvergenceofatleast 50% of the cases, the procedure is stopped and no result is provided. Post-hoc analyses andcomparisons between groups were performed using the Duncan test.
Subsequently, multiple linear regression analyses (forward method) were conducted onthetotalorganiccarbon,soilorganicmatter,carbonstockandsoildensitycontentsindifferenttreatments. All tests were performed using IBM® SPSS® software (SPSS CORP, 2017),considering results with p < 0.05 as statistically significant.

This variability can be attributed to the differences between MT and NT. Minimumtillage, which involves lower soil disturbance, tends to maintain a more intact soil structure,butwithgreater soildisturbancecompared toNT, resultinginlower densityvalues.
It is also important to note that, since corn is grown in a tropical climate, organic matterdecomposesmorequicklyinConventionaltillage(CT)duetogreatersoildisturbance,especially in the upper layers (0 – 0.10 m) (FUJII et al., 2022). In tropical regions, hightemperatures accelerate the decomposition of organic matter when it is exposed on the soilsurface. This decomposition process reduces the amount of organic matter that would beavailable to improve the colloidal structure of the soil (SHAHZAD et al., 2021). Thus, greatersoil exposure due to more invasive cropping practices can lead to fast mineralization oforganicmatter, reducing thesoil's ability to formstable aggregates.
On the other hand, in Conventional tillage (CT), soil density in the 0-10 cm layer washigher for all previous crops treatmenrs, with Millet having a statistically significant loweraverage soil density (1.622 gcm³) while sunn hemp had the highest statistically significantaverage density among the previous crops (1.631 gcm³). Other previous crops did not presentsignificantdifferences (Table 2).
Itwasobservedthat,forthedepthof0.10–0.20m,No-Tillage(NT)alsopresentedhigh soil density values compared to the depth of 0.0 – 0.10 m, especially when associatedwithcropssuchasmillet,whichpresentedanaveragedensityof1.822gcm³.However,unlike the most superficial layer, the soil density in NT for the depth of 0.10 – 0.20 m did notpresent statistically significant variations between the different previous crops, indicating asmaller influence of these on soil compaction in this depth range.
In the case of Minimum tillage (MT), there was a statistically significant increase in soildensity at a depth of 0.10 – 0.20 m, especially when associated with millet, which recorded anaverage density of 1.954 gcm³, followed by Cowpea with 1.761 gcm³. This initial reductionin compaction is associated with the minimum soil plow of from the use of light implementsovertheyears,atadepthof0.30m(LAMANDÉ;SCHJØNNING,2011),exertingareductionin the breakdown of soil aggregates even at this depth (up to 0.20 m) (PULIDO-MONCADA;MUNKHOLM; SCHJØNNING, 2019). This behavior can be confirmed by the similaritybetweenMinimumtillage(MT)andConventionaltillage(CT),withlesspronouncedvariations between the different previous crops, but with a soil density slightly lower than thatobservedin NT.
These results indicate that the depth of 0.10–0.20 m is less susceptible to variationscaused by previous crops, being more influenced by the type of used tillage system. Thus,tillage systems that minimize soil disturbance tend to maintain a higher density at greaterdepths, contributing to soil compaction and reduction of soil porosity, which can impact waterinfiltrationand rootdevelopment (WANGetal., 2019).
Tillage systems with reduced or no soil disturbance tend to produce more cohesivelayers on the surface. Denardin et al., (2019) observed similar results, where NT conditionedby crop rotation presented the highest density values when compared to the other systems, CTand MT. Furthermore, conservation systems such as NT reduce the susceptibility of the soil tosealing and surface degradation, improving the stability of aggregates against disaggregationforces(GRANDINETTI;CANTEROMARTÍNEZ;RAMOS,2022),whichcanpromotegreater soil density in the most superficial layers (SHAHGHOLI et al., 2023).

Organic carbon in soil


At depths of 0.0 – 0.10 m and 0.10 – 0.20 m, the analysis of variance showed asignificanteffectofthesoiltillagesystem[F(2,96)=34.348;p<0.001]and[F(2,96)=66.652; p < 0.001], presented respectively. The effect size was moderate, with η² = 0.417 andη² = 0.581 at the studied depths. The data indicate that the different studied tillage systemsexplain 41.7% of the variance of organic carbon at the depth of 0.0 – 0.10 m and 58.1% at thedepth of 0.10 – 0.20 m (Figure 2).
Considering the previous crops, the analysis of variance showed that the total organiccarbon in the soil had a significant effect on the soil for the depth of 0.0 – 0.10 m [ F (3, 96) =10.838; p < 0.001] but with a low power of effect (η²) of 0.253, indicating that the previouscrops explain 25.3% of the variance of organic carbon at this depth. The interaction betweenthe tillage system and the previous crops was also significant [ F (6, 96) = 8.886; p < 0.001],with a low power of effect (η²) of 0.357, suggesting that this interaction explains 35.7% of thevarianceof organic carbon.
The observed data indicate that the different soil tillage systems considering the of corncrop were important for the deposition of soil organic carbon, especially in subsurface layers(0.10 to 0.20 m), explaining up to 58.1% of the observed variance. The influence of previouscrops, although significant, is less pronounced, explaining 25.3% of the variance of organiccarbonin the surface layer.
The interaction between these factors, soil tillage and previous crops, is also relevant,contributingto35.7%ofthevariance,suggestingthatthecombinationofmanagementpractices and crop choice can be strategic to maximize carbon retention in the soil. Thisgreaterimpactofsoiltillageindeeperlayerssuggeststhatlong-termsoilmanagementpractices are crucial for carbon retention, particularly in tropical regions (COOPER et al.,2021).Thedecompositionoforganicmatterisacceleratedwhensubjectedtohightemperatures, where the greater concentration of OM in the upper layers promotes thermalstabilityin deeper layers(WANG, et al.,2020).
Furthermore, the fast decomposition of organic matter in tropical climates is driven byhigh microbial activity favored by high temperatures, increasing the rate of mineralization oforganic carbon and its release as CO. Under these conditions, the exposure of organic matteron the soil surface, resulting from soil tillage practices, promotes greater mineralization ofnutrients and increases the consumption of available carbon stocks (LEIZEAGA et al., 2022).Soil carbon stabilization is also influenced by geological and environmental interactions thatcontrol the dynamics of organic carbon (SCHMIDT et al., 2011), explaining the interactionsexogenous to the studied variables.
To obtain more significant depositions of soil organic carbon by previous crops, thissystemmustbeassociatedwithconservationistsoiltillagesystemsthatminimizetheexposure of organic matter and promote the formation of stable aggregates (DOETTERL etal., 2015). Among the previous crops, considering only no-till, sunn hemp and pigeon pea presentedthe highest averages of organic carbon at the superficial depth (0.0 – 0.10 m), withaverage values of 0.798 and 0.645 gkg, respectively (Figure 2). At a depth of 0.10 – 0.20 m,the values were 0.426 g/kg and 0.469 gkg, respectively. Furthermore, the use of sunn hemp in association with poaceae tends to release nutrientsmoreslowly.Plantmorphology,rootdevelopment,andnitrogenfixationcapacityarecommonfactors that contribute to sunn hemp and cowpea presenting a greater accumulation of TotalOrganicCarbon in the soil,whether on the surface orsubsurface (SIMUNJI et al.,2019).

CONCLUSION

No-tillage has proven to be an effective conservation system for increasing organiccarbon content in the soil, promoting particle aggregation due to reduced disturbance andenhancedstabilityoforganicmatter.Inminimumtillagesystems,soildensityvariessignificantly according to previous crops, with legumes such as cowpea and pigeon peashowingloweraveragesoildensities,whereasmilletexhibitsthehighestdensities.Amongthe studied previous crops, sunn hemp and pigeon pea stood out as those that contributed mostto higher organic carbon levels in the analyzed soil layers. These findings underscore theimportanceofusinglegumesascovercrops,giventheirnitrogen-fixingcapabilitiesanddeeproot development, which facilitate the accumulation of organic carbon. The adoption of soilmanagement systems that minimize disturbance, such as minimum tillage and no-tillage,combined with the use of legumes as cover crops, can maximize carbon retention and increasesoil quality for green corn production. Sustaining these practices over time is essential toensurethesustainabilityandproductivityofagriculturalsystems,particularlyintropicalregionswhere thedecomposition of organicmatter occursrapidly.
